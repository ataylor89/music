# The joy of classical music

## Introduction

I love music... and it's no secret.

I listen to all kinds of music -- baroque, classical, contemporary, and Christian.

I remember, as a high school student, having an earnest desire to get into classical music. But it was difficult. I listened to classical music, but it was hard to understand.

Years later, after I graduated college, I became friends with someone who enjoys classical music and listens to it often.

We attended the symphony together and heard Beethoven's 6th symphony.

I was enchanted by Beethoven's 6th symphony.

I listened to his other symphonies and was enchanted by them as well.

I started to listen to music by Beethoven, Mozart, Bach, Handel, Pachelbel, Shostakovich, Rachmaninoff, and Tchaikovsky.

My friend gave me this advice: "Listen for the themes".

I discovered that one way to learn a piece is to learn its themes.

This was all in 2014 or 2015 (probably 2015).

Years later, my family taught me that themes are the heart of classical music.

"Classical music is very thematic," my family said.

"Themes are the heart of classical music. A symphony explores a theme."

"Counterpoint is the heart of baroque music."

What is a theme? you might ask.

What is a motif? What is a melody?

What is harmony? What is counterpoint?

These are good questions. Let's answer these questions with a vocabulary sheet.

Music vocabulary:

1. Theme: (1) A musical idea that develops or repeats throughout a piece (2) A melody that repeats throughout a piece
2. Motif: I think that a motif is just a theme. In my opinion, the two words are synonymous and interchangeable, although they might have slightly different connotations.
3. Melody: A sequence of notes; a sequence of notes that creates a musical tune.
4. Harmony: A musical technique in which two or more notes are played simultaneously.
5. Counterpoint: (1) A musical technique in which two or more melodies are played simultaneously (2) Interwoven melodies in a musical composition (source: Scripps National Spelling Bee)

It also helps to have some examples.

In the second movement of Beethoven's seventh symphony, there is a clear theme that is played over and over again in low notes. Eventually, it is accompanied by a melody, and the theme and the melody are played together in counterpoint.

The second movement of Beethoven's seventh symphony provides us with an example of a musical theme, and it also provides us with an example of counterpoint.

Another example would be the Do Re Fa Mi theme in Mozart's first symphony and also in his fourty first symphony, which is often called the Jupiter symphony.

The Do Re Fa Mi theme appears in the fourth movement of Mozart's fourty first symphony. The fourth movement of the 41st also makes use of counterpoint.

Another example would be the Ode to Joy theme in Beethoven's ninth symphony. This theme gets played over and over again in the fourth movement of the symphony.

Beethoven's third symphony (Eroica) has many memorable themes. I set the lyrics "I'll stand tall and I will not fall" to a theme in the first movement of the symphony.

As you can see, classical music is very thematic.

Beethoven's work and Mozart's work are replete with musical themes.

I remember going to Beethoven's 6th symphony with my friend in 2014 or 2015.

His advice to me was, "listen to the themes" or "listen for the themes".

I took this advice to heart. It really helped.

I listened to many works of classical music, after that, and I studied the pieces, and I researched the pieces, and I figured out what were the main themes of each piece.

I discovered many pieces that I love, by Beethoven, Mozart, Bach, Handel, Pachelbel, Shostakovich, Rachmaninoff, Tchaikovsky, and many other composers.

I paid close attention to the themes in each piece.
